The True Counter to the New Abnormal

A Response to “The internet didn’t kill counter culture — you just won’t find it on Instagram”

Is nothingness still today's counter-culture?

By Alfredo Morales

Caroline Busta just like Kyle Chayka gives us compelling points about how we live our life and what we consider counter culture nowadays. Busta, using complex language, criticizes the hierarchical structure social media and corporations has established over time. Digital platforms have transformed counter cultural activity into profitable, high-engagement content, being counter cultural no longer means being counter-hegemonic or counter corporations. However, unlike Chyaka, Busta offers possible solutions, where Chyaka sees a problem in minimalism, Busta sees true power. Busta explains how the power of today is firmly situated in minimalism, restraint, and ease. She states how actual power is controlling the means by which lesser power can be displayed. Even though you may have 20,000 followers on Instagram, Zuckerbug is still the owner of your online profile, you have limited power. Actual power keeps a low profile and doesn’t need a social media presence.

Is nothingness still today's counter-culture?

As I stated earlier, in Chayka’s response to nothingness, she catalogs it as something we are prisoner’s to, and becoming more attracted to, something negative. Busta, however, sees it as a possible solution, true counter culture is hidden in the nothingness. I agree with the author's assessment of where counterculture resides. Personally, I have explored the dark forests of Reddit and it’s autonomy. As Busta describes true counter culture is not interested in being seen—at least not in person. It gets no thrill out of wearing leather and a mohawk & walking past main-street shops. But it does demonstrate a hunger for freedom—freedom from the attention economy, from atomization, and the extractive logic of mainstream communication. We can imagine a proliferation of digital gangs in dark forests who hold secrets dear, and a new desire for scarcity in cultural objects—deeper and closer connections made between people even while rejecting the platform’s compulsion to “like and share.” In the internet era.
