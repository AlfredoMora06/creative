Living in the New Abnormal

A Response to “How Nothingness Became Everything We Wanted”

Can nothingness be this century's rebellion? A possible counter-culture.

By Alfredo Morales

Kyle Chayka gives us a compelling read, making us question this new normal life we are currently living that would’ve been deemed abnormal a year or two ago. Nowadays, news outlets and social media focus on constantly giving us headlines, quotes and information of all kinds. We try to find escape from this noise and static that is always wanting our attention, some of us by unplugging, others by escaping physically. As Chaykaetz goes on, I recall a content creator known as Matt D’ Avela and how he has grown his YouTube channel to over three million subscribers through minimalism (the idea of living with less). At first I see nothing wrong with this lifestyle of having less materialistic possessions and living through experiences. Although, as Chaykaetz argues, “This obsession with absence, the intentional erasure of self and surroundings, is the apotheosis of what I’ve come to think of as a culture of negation: a body of cultural output, from material goods to entertainment franchises to lifestyle fads, that evinces a desire to reject the overstimulation that defines contemporary existence.”

Chaykaetz doesn’t give us a solution as there probably isn’t one, he provides a Nietzsche-type perspective on us. He states a problem, that the search for nothingness has led us nowhere, and in fact, has only imprisoned us more in this bubble that we might call home at times - stuck with our amazon wishlist, netflix binge list, seamless seemingly limitless pizza orders and video games. However, there are different perspectives in the way we can look at things. Maybe nothingness might not be entirely bad, nothing is absolute in the world when it comes to complicated concepts. Chaykaetz swims into shallow waters with the concept of companies and how they use information for profit. This is where the idea of Big Data comes in. Big Data is known as the ability of society to harness information in novel ways to produce useful insights or goods and services of significant value. Data today is no longer regarded as static or stale, whose usefulness was finished once the purpose for which it was collected was achieved. Data has become a raw material of business, a vital economic input, used to create a new form of economic value.

As our digital universe only expands with time, to unimaginable amounts. Big Data is used by companies to gain more wealth and power at the expense of its users. This leads to an issue in privacy. GPS technology and smartphone apps take note of what we buy, where and when, how much money we have in the bank, whom we text/e-mail, what we visit, how and where we travel, what time we go to sleep and wake up, etc. Maybe the act of nothingness, total unplugging should be seen as an act of rebellion, counter-culture, a movement to take control of our life again. If that’s the case we should definitely strive to be consistent in what we aren’t using, not just social media, and not to escape life but go back to enjoying it.
